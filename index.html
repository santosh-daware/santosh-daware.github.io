Research Themes

Soft Matter

Synthesis, Characterization, and Applications of Colloidal Analogues of 2D Materials
Sheet-like colloidal assemblies are model systems to study two-dimensional materials. We present a method to prepare these assemblies with controlled size, crystalline order, flexibility, and defect density by evaporating a colloidal dispersion on an oil-covered substrate. Crosslinking the polymer makes the assemblies permanent, with ordered structures forming at higher concentrations and disordered ones at lower concentrations. Varying the crosslinker ratio affects flexibility, and introducing different-sized colloids or sacrificial particles creates disorder and holes, respectively, allowing for studies on the effects of doping and vacancies.

Pickering Emulsions and Bijels Stabilized by Shape Anisotropic Particles using Arrested Demixing
A binary liquid–liquid mixture with a lower critical solution temperature (LCST) undergoes demixing when heated above a critical temperature, creating high-energy liquid–liquid interfaces before phase separation occurs. By adding colloidal silica rods to a homogeneous lutidine/water (L/W) mixture before phase separation, we achieve colloidal rod-stabilized Pickering emulsions, with droplet sizes tunable by particle concentration. Shape anisotropic particles, like hematite ellipsoids, stabilize fluid-fluid interfaces more effectively due to capillary interactions and geometric effects, allowing for the stabilization of non-spherical emulsions. This method of using hematite particles to arrest temperature-induced demixing in L/W mixtures holds potential for applications in electrochemical systems and catalyst technology.



Sustainability and Urban Mining

Recovery of Rare Earth Metals from Electronic Waste
We demonstrate a protocol for recovering Dy and Nd from used NdFeB magnets through crushing, roasting, grinding, leaching, liquid–liquid extraction, and stripping. Roasting and leaching conditions were optimized to selectively leach Nd and Dy, removing most Fe as Fe(OH)3. Dy and Nd were then extracted using D2EHPA in a two-stage process, with Dy extracted first at pH 2 and Nd at pH 4, and both metals recovered from the organic phase with HCl. This project was sponsored by CEFIPRA and CSIR-NML , ICSM France , and Terra Nova Development, France as collaborators. We conducted field pilot trials with a scaled-up continuous mode of operations, showcasing the potential for recovering rare earth elements from electronic waste, providing an alternative to conventional mining.

A Machine Learning Framework for Metal Recovery from Electronic Waste
Numerous research articles have developed methods for recovering metals from secondary sources, but these methods often lack reproducibility due to variations in source composition. To address this, a modeling framework was developed to design a copper recovery process from printed circuit boards (PCBs), using data from 23 research articles visualized in four-dimensional plots. Five supervised machine-learning algorithms were trained and tested, with the gradient boost machine model performing best, achieving a mean absolute error (MAE) of 10.83% and an F1 score of 0.72. Feature importance analysis was conducted, revealing key parameters for high recovery, and the framework is adaptable for designing various recovery processes.



Formulation Design

Development of Functional Coatings for Glass and Metal Surfaces
Coating formulation design requires precise component quantities to balance various functionalities, which often have counter effects. This study employs a data-driven approach with minimal experiments to design a hydrophobic silica-alumina hybrid coating, analyzing the impact of nanoparticle concentration, PTFE polymer percentage, and spin coating speed on characteristics like contact angle, roll-off angle, scratch hardness, adhesion, and thickness. A combination of design of experiments and machine learning was used to optimize a formulation that enhances scratch hardness without sacrificing transparency, utilizing a commercial clearcoat resin and in-situ ball milling to create nanoparticles. The study explored alumina, silica, and titania nanoparticles and characterized the coatings for thickness, transparency, scratch hardness, and adhesion.

Nanocoolant for Automobile Engine Cooling
The automobile industry aims to enhance engine efficiency while meeting emission standards, with a focus on engine coolant temperature's effect on fuel efficiency and emissions. This study developed a high-energy milling process to produce stable nanofluids from commercial coolants and compared their performance to traditional coolants. Tests on a diesel engine revealed that nanocoolants improve the log mean temperature difference (LMTD) by 2-3% and reduce brake specific fuel consumption and oxygen in the exhaust. We scaled up the production of nanocoolant.
